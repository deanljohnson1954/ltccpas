State Mandated LTC Insurance

Understanding state LTC programs, payroll taxes, opt-out options, and what it means for your planning.

The New Landscape of State LTC Mandates

For decades, long-term care insurance was entirely a matter of individual choice. That is changing. States across the country are beginning to follow Washington State's landmark example — creating government-funded LTC programs financed by mandatory payroll taxes on employees. Understanding these mandates is now a critical component of financial planning for working Americans.

Washington State: The WA Cares Fund — The First State Mandate

Washington State was the first state in the nation to implement a mandatory LTC insurance program. The WA Cares Fund, which took effect in 2023, imposes a payroll tax of 0.58% on all wages earned in Washington. For a worker earning $100,000 per year, that's $580 per year — every year — taken automatically from their paycheck.

In exchange, qualified employees receive LTC benefits of up to $36,500 (indexed for inflation) over their lifetime if they need long-term care. This sounds helpful on the surface — but the limitations are significant:

  • Benefits are capped at approximately $36,500 lifetime — compared to typical private LTC costs of $200,000 to $1.8 million
  • Benefits only apply to Washington State residents — if you move away after retirement, you forfeit the benefits you paid for
  • No benefits are paid if you didn't work in Washington long enough to vest (10 years or 3 of the last 6 years)
  • The benefit covers only a fraction of typical nursing home or memory care costs

The Opt-Out Provision

Washington State provided a one-time opt-out opportunity for employees who could demonstrate they had qualified private LTC insurance coverage before the deadline. Those who successfully opted out are permanently exempt from the payroll tax for their Washington employment.

The opt-out window in Washington has closed, but it established an important precedent: private LTC insurance can exempt you from state LTC payroll taxes. This precedent will almost certainly carry forward as other states implement similar programs.

Other States Considering LTC Mandates

Following Washington's lead, a number of states are actively developing or studying their own mandatory LTC programs:

  • California: The California Long Term Care Task Force, created by AB 567, has developed recommendations for a state LTC program. California — with its large workforce and high care costs — could implement one of the most significant state LTC payroll taxes in the country.
  • New York, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Minnesota, Hawaii, Illinois: All have had active legislative discussions or task forces studying state LTC funding mechanisms.
  • Oregon: Has studied the Washington model and explored similar programs.

The trend is clear: state LTC mandates are coming. States are recognizing that the cost of providing Medicaid LTC benefits is unsustainable, and they are shifting the funding burden to workers through payroll taxes.

Why Private LTC Insurance Is Now More Important Than Ever

State mandates actually make the case for private LTC insurance stronger, not weaker, for several reasons:

  • Tax savings: If your state implements an opt-out provision tied to private coverage, having a qualifying LTC policy could save you thousands in payroll taxes over your career.
  • Superior coverage: State programs provide minimal benefits — private policies provide comprehensive, lifetime coverage tailored to your needs and the actual cost of care in your area.
  • Flexibility: Private policies cover care anywhere — not just in the state where you paid taxes.
  • Guaranteed death benefit option: Asset-based private LTC policies return your investment as a death benefit if care is never needed. State programs do not.

The California Situation

California's Long Term Care Task Force has recommended a payroll tax-funded program similar to Washington's WA Cares Fund. Given California's notoriously high cost of living and care costs — Bay Area nursing home costs regularly exceed $20,000 to $24,000 per month — state benefits, if implemented, would cover only a small fraction of actual costs.

If California implements a mandate, the opt-out window could be narrow, demand for private LTC insurance will surge, and premiums will increase. Purchasing private LTC coverage now — while you are younger and healthier — is the best way to lock in favorable rates and potentially be positioned to opt out of future state programs.

The optimal strategy: purchase a qualifying private LTC policy now, while premiums are lower and your health is stronger. If and when your state implements an opt-out provision, you'll be positioned to avoid the payroll tax while maintaining comprehensive coverage that far exceeds any state program.
