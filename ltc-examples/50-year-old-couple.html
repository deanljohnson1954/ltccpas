Long-Term Care Insurance for a 50-Year-Old Couple

One hybrid policy, three outcomes — and a guaranteed financial win no matter which one occurs.

Consider a married couple, both age 50, in good health. In the Bay Area, long-term care runs roughly $15,000 per month per person — about $180,000 a year each, or $360,000 a year if both need care at once. The question is not whether a family like this can afford the first year of care. It is who pays for year three, year five, or year ten.

A single-premium hybrid life and long-term care policy answers that question while guaranteeing the family comes out ahead in every scenario. Here is how the numbers work for this couple.

How the Policy Works

A single premium funds lifetime protection for both spouses

A one-time premium of $203,900 fully funds a hybrid policy covering both insureds — no further premiums are ever due. The policy pays up to $15,000 per month per insured for long-term care, for life — there is no time limit and no benefit cap on how long care is covered. It also builds guaranteed cash value from day one, which the couple can borrow against, and if long-term care is never needed, the full $360,000 death benefit passes to the family. There is no “use it or lose it.”

A Guaranteed Win — Three Ways

However the future unfolds, the family is ahead

If care is never needed: Beneficiaries receive the $360,000 death benefit — $156,100 more than the $203,900 premium paid. A guaranteed gain, income-tax-free under current law.

If care is needed: The policy pays $30,000 per month jointly, for life. In a severe case — both spouses needing care from age 50 — the policy could pay roughly $12.6 million by age 85, many times the premium.

If plans change: The policy builds a guaranteed cash value — starting around $80,223 — that the couple can borrow against or surrender at any time.

The Cash-Flow Analysis

What the protection actually costs

The headline premium overstates the true cost, because most of it is recoverable as cash value or returns as a death benefit. The real measure is net capital at risk:

“The net cost of lifetime protection — about $123,677 — is roughly four months of joint Bay Area care. Self-insuring ‘saves’ that amount, but leaves the family responsible for every dollar of care thereafter. A single extended event would dwarf it.”

The Tax Dimension

Where the structure pays off

Of the $203,900 premium, $121,676 is the long-term care portion. For a C-Corporation, that portion is generally deductible — a tax benefit of up to roughly $36,590 at combined federal (21%) and California (8.84%) rates. For individuals, deductibility of the long-term care portion is subject to the IRS age-based eligible-premium limits rather than the full amount. In all cases, long-term care benefits paid under a qualified policy are generally received income-tax-free.

Same coverage — funded to fit the family’s cash flow

Each structure below provides the identical $360,000 face amount and $15,000 monthly benefit, with premiums guaranteed never to increase. The difference is simply how the cost is paid over time — families typically choose based on cash flow, liquid assets, and time horizon to retirement.

The Bottom Line

Protection that pays either way

For about $124,000 of net capital at risk — less for a business owner — this couple secures lifetime long-term care coverage with no cap, a guaranteed cash value they can borrow against, and a $360,000 death benefit if care is never needed. It is not an insurance expense to be minimized; it is a planning decision about how, and when, to set aside capital that protects the family either way.
