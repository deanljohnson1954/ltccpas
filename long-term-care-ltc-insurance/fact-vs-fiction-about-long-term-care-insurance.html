Fact vs. Fiction About LTC Insurance

Separating the myths from reality so you can make informed decisions about your long-term care protection.

Misconceptions about long-term care insurance are widespread — and costly. Many people make decisions based on things they've heard or assumed, rather than the actual facts. Below, we address the most common myths we encounter from clients every day.

Fiction: Medicare covers long-term care.

Fact: Medicare covers only short-term skilled nursing care — and only under specific conditions. After a qualifying hospital stay of at least 3 days, Medicare may cover up to 100 days of skilled nursing facility care, but coverage is limited (and often ends much sooner). Medicare does not cover custodial care — the assistance with bathing, dressing, and daily activities that constitutes the vast majority of long-term care needs. This is one of the most dangerous and persistent misconceptions in financial planning.

Fiction: I'm too young to worry about LTC insurance.

Fact: Over 40% of people receiving long-term care services are between the ages of 18 and 64. Accidents, strokes, neurological conditions, and early-onset dementia can strike at any age. Beyond the risk side, purchasing LTC insurance in your 50s is dramatically less expensive than waiting until your 60s or 70s. Youth is actually the strongest argument for acting now — not for waiting.

Fiction: I'll self-insure by using my savings.

Fact: Self-insuring sounds financially sophisticated, but the math is often brutal. A 10-year dementia episode in a memory care facility can cost $1.8 million or more. To fund that from savings in a 40% tax bracket requires earning approximately $3 million gross. And that assumes the savings hold — which means no market downturns, no inflation, and no competing financial needs. Most families discover, too late, that they vastly underestimated what self-insurance actually requires.

Fiction: I won't qualify because of my health.

Fact: Most people in reasonably good health will qualify for at least some form of LTC coverage. While certain conditions — like Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, or certain recent strokes — can affect eligibility, the underwriting landscape is broader than most people assume. Asset-based and hybrid LTC policies sometimes have more flexible underwriting than traditional stand-alone LTC policies. The only way to know is to apply. Many of our clients who feared they wouldn't qualify have found excellent options available to them.

Fiction: LTC insurance is too expensive.

Fact: LTC insurance can be structured to fit virtually any budget. The two main levers are the monthly benefit amount and the benefit period — both of which can be adjusted to find a premium you can comfortably afford. Additionally, single-premium asset-based policies allow a one-time lump-sum deposit that generates substantial LTC coverage — without ongoing premium payments. Many clients find that the "cost" of the policy is actually less than the cost of self-insuring for even one year of care.

Fiction: If I never use it, the premiums are wasted.

Fact: This was true of traditional LTC policies — and was one of their most common criticisms. Modern asset-based (hybrid) LTC insurance solves this completely. With a hybrid policy, if LTC is never needed, the death benefit paid to your heirs equals or exceeds the total premiums paid. There is no "waste" — you either receive LTC benefits during your lifetime, or your heirs receive a death benefit. Either way, your money generates value.

Fiction: My family will take care of me.

Fact: Many people assume their children will provide care if they need it. The reality is more complicated. Professional long-term care — particularly for advanced conditions requiring skilled nursing — is beyond the ability of most family members to provide. And even for the care that families can provide, the physical, emotional, and financial toll on caregivers is enormous. Having proper LTC insurance protects your family members from becoming unpaid caregivers, preserves family relationships, and gives everyone more choices.

Fiction: I can buy LTC insurance when I need it.

Fact: LTC insurance cannot be purchased after a need for care arises or after a condition develops that would require LTC. The time to buy is when you are healthy enough to qualify. By the time most people realize they need LTC coverage, it's often too late to obtain it at favorable rates — or at all. The underwriting is done at the time of application, and any condition that develops afterward does not affect an existing policy (but would block a new application).
