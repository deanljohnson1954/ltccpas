The Insurance You Are Most Likely to Need — And Least Likely to Have

A financial case for long-term care planning that the numbers make for themselves.

Most Americans carry insurance for events they hope never happen and statistically may never face. They insure their automobiles against accidents that, for most drivers, will never occur — or if they do, will resolve in a matter of days and cost thousands of dollars, not hundreds of thousands. They insure their homes against fires that, in the overwhelming majority of cases, will never materialize.

Yet the majority of American families — including many financially sophisticated professionals — carry no protection against the one risk that is, by any actuarial measure, more likely to occur than most of the events they routinely insure against: the need for long-term care.

This is not an oversight that can be explained by ignorance of the risk. It is, more often, the result of underestimating it — a combination of optimism, avoidance, and the mistaken belief that Medicare, personal savings, or family members will absorb the cost when the time comes. None of these assumptions holds up under serious analysis.

The Likelihood

What the Statistics Actually Show

The foundational question in any insurance decision is probability: how likely is this event to occur?

For long-term care, the answer is unambiguous. According to data from the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, approximately 70 percent of Americans who reach age 65 will require some form of long-term care before the end of their lives. That is not a marginal risk. It is not a tail event. It is the actuarial expectation for the majority of the population.

Compare this to the risks that American households insure as a matter of course:

  • The probability of a house fire severe enough to cause significant damage in any given year is estimated at less than one percent.
  • The likelihood of a serious automobile accident resulting in a claim varies by driver, but the probability of a total loss or major injury claim in any given year is a small fraction of the driving population.
  • The chance of a significant theft or liability event is similarly low.

Long-term care, by contrast, carries a 70 percent lifetime probability for those who reach retirement age — and it is routinely left unaddressed.

The asymmetry is striking. Families insure confidently against risks with a 1 to 5 percent annual probability of occurrence, and leave unprotected a risk that is, by a wide margin, more likely than not.

The Cost

Why Self-Insurance Is Not the Strategy It Appears to Be

Probability alone does not complete the picture. The severity of the potential loss matters equally. Long-term care sits firmly in the high-probability, high-severity category.

Professional home health care in the Bay Area commonly ranges from $8,000 to $15,000 per month, depending on hours of care, the level of acuity involved, and the composition of the care team.

Assisted living facilities in the Bay Area range from $7,000 to $12,000 or more per month. These costs are borne entirely by the resident or their family — Medicare does not cover them.

Memory care facilities for individuals with dementia or Alzheimer’s disease regularly exceed $12,000 to $15,000 per month in the Bay Area, with many top-tier facilities commanding rates above that range.

“At $12,500 per month, a five-year care event represents $750,000 in direct care costs. A ten-year event — by no means unusual for advanced dementia — doubles that exposure to $1.5 million. This is a quantifiable retirement risk, not an abstract concern.”

No automobile accident, house fire, or theft event approaches these magnitudes. The financial severity of a long-term care event, for most families, is categorically different from the risks they routinely insure against.

The Duration

Why Long-Term Care Is Unlike Any Other Insurable Risk

Long-term care is distinctive not only in its probability and cost, but in its structure. Most insurable risks are episodic — they occur, they resolve, and the financial exposure concludes. Long-term care is not episodic. It is sustained.

The average duration of a long-term care event exceeds three years. For dementia-related care, the average duration exceeds five years, and many cases extend well beyond ten. Unlike a hospital stay or a surgical recovery, dementia is progressive, not resolving.

A care event that begins at $10,000 per month and continues for eight years generates cumulative costs approaching $1,000,000 — even before accounting for care cost inflation. The duration risk is the most underappreciated dimension of the long-term care equation.

The Coverage Gap

What Medicare Does Not Do

A significant portion of the planning gap in long-term care can be traced to a persistent misconception: the belief that Medicare will cover the cost. Medicare is an acute care program — it does not cover the sustained custodial care that long-term care events require.

Medicare may cover a limited stay in a skilled nursing facility following a qualifying hospitalization: up to 100 days, with the first 20 days covered in full and substantial co-payments thereafter. Beyond that, coverage ends entirely.

Medicare does not cover:

  • Ongoing home health aide services for custodial care
  • Assisted living facilities
  • Memory care facilities
  • Adult day programs
  • Personal care assistance with activities of daily living

Medi-Cal does cover long-term care, but only after most personal assets have been depleted to program eligibility levels. For professionals with meaningful retirement savings, Medi-Cal is not a plan. It is the financial outcome that planning is designed to prevent.

The Underwriting Reality

The Window Closes Faster Than Most Families Expect

Long-term care insurance requires medical underwriting. The ability to obtain it diminishes with age and declining health — often before families recognize the urgency.

The ideal planning window — when coverage is available across the broadest range of carriers, at the most favorable premiums — is typically in a client’s fifties and early sixties. By the mid-seventies, many carriers will not issue new long-term care policies at all.

Families who wait until care appears imminent frequently find that the coverage they waited too long to purchase is no longer available to them. This is the one aspect of long-term care planning that has no remedy. The underwriting window, once closed, does not reopen.

A Distinctive Planning Advantage

A Design That Returns Value Even When Care Is Never Needed

One of the most common objections to long-term care coverage is the perception that premiums paid over many years may be lost if care is never required. This objection has a direct structural answer.

Certain hybrid life and long-term care insurance designs are structured so that if long-term care benefits are never used, a death benefit passes to the policyholder’s beneficiaries. The premiums are not lost. The policy functions as both protection against a care event and as a component of a broader estate plan.

In one recent engagement, a thorough multi-carrier review resulted in a policy structure that provided $3.8 million in guaranteed death benefit — at no material increase in premiums relative to the client’s prior coverage. That outcome was the result of independent, carrier-neutral analysis applied with the rigor of an accounting professional, not a product salesperson.

Putting It in Perspective

The Comparison That Reframes the Decision

A driver purchases automobile insurance — paying premiums year after year — because the alternative, an uninsured loss, is financially unacceptable. The probability of a major claim in any given year is low. The premium is paid anyway.

Apply the same logic to long-term care. The probability is not low. It is 70 percent. The cost is not measured in thousands of dollars — it is measured in hundreds of thousands, and for extended dementia cases, in millions. The duration is not days or weeks — it is years, sometimes a decade or more.

“The case for long-term care coverage is not sentimental. It is arithmetical. The numbers that define this risk belong in every serious retirement analysis — not as a footnote, but as a primary planning variable.”
