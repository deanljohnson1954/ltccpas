Frequently Asked Questions

Concise answers to the questions clients and their CPAs ask most often about long-term care insurance, underwriting, benefits, and planning structures.

The Basics

Q:  What exactly is long-term care insurance?

Long-term care insurance pays for the professional assistance people need when they can no longer perform basic activities of daily living (ADLs) — such as bathing, dressing, eating, toileting, or transferring — or when they require supervision due to cognitive impairment. It covers care in the home, assisted living facilities, memory care units, adult day programs, and nursing homes.

Q:  How likely am I to actually need long-term care?

Very likely. According to U.S. Department of Health and Human Services data, approximately 70% of Americans who reach age 65 will need some form of long-term care before the end of their lives. One in five will need care for five years or longer. This is not a marginal risk — it is the actuarial expectation for the majority of the population.

Q:  Won’t Medicare cover my long-term care costs?

No. Medicare is an acute care program. It covers hospitalizations, physician services, and short-term skilled nursing care after a qualifying hospital stay — limited to 100 days, with significant co-pays after day 20. It does not cover ongoing custodial care, assisted living, memory care, adult day programs, or home health aide services for non-medical needs. This is one of the most common and costly misconceptions in retirement planning.

Q:  What does long-term care actually cost in California?

At 2026 California rates: a home health aide (44 hrs/week) runs approximately $6,200/month; assisted living $5,100/month; memory care $6,200/month; nursing home semi-private $9,000/month; nursing home private room $10,500/month. A three-year nursing home stay exceeds $315,000. A ten-year dementia event can approach $1.5 million.

Coverage & Benefits

Q:  What does a long-term care policy actually pay for?

A qualified LTC policy pays for care in whatever setting is needed: professional home health aide services, assisted living facilities, memory care units, adult day programs, nursing homes, and hospice care. Claims data shows 53% of claims are for home health aides and 60% of all claims involve home-based or skilled nursing home visits — confirming that most people receive care where they prefer to be.

Q:  What triggers a long-term care claim?

A qualified LTC policy is triggered when an insured cannot perform two or more of the six Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) — bathing, dressing, eating, toileting, transferring, and continence — or when they require substantial supervision due to severe cognitive impairment such as Alzheimer’s disease or dementia. A licensed healthcare practitioner must certify that the condition is expected to last at least 90 days.

Q:  What is an elimination period?

The elimination period is a waiting period — typically 30, 60, or 90 days — that must pass after a qualifying care event begins before the policy pays benefits. It functions like a deductible measured in time rather than dollars. A 90-day elimination period is the most common and generally produces the most cost-effective premium structure.

Q:  How long do benefits last?

Policy benefit periods typically range from two years to lifetime, or are expressed as a pool of money (e.g., $300,000 total benefit pool). Shorter benefit periods reduce premiums but increase the risk of outliving coverage. Given that 20% of claimants needed care for five or more years, and dementia cases frequently extend beyond ten years, benefit period selection is one of the most consequential design decisions.

Q:  What is inflation protection and do I need it?

Inflation protection causes your benefit to increase over time — typically 3% or 5% compounded annually. Care costs have historically outpaced general inflation. A policy purchased at age 55 may not pay a claim for 25 years. Without inflation protection, the real value of the benefit erodes from the day the policy is issued. For most clients still in their fifties and sixties, some form of inflation protection is essential.

Underwriting & Eligibility

Q:  What does underwriting involve?

LTC underwriting assesses your health history, current medical conditions, medications, cognitive function, and actuarial risk. Carriers review medical records, conduct telephone or in-person interviews, and in some cases require cognitive assessments or physical examinations. The process typically takes two to six weeks from application to decision.

Q:  What health conditions can affect my ability to get coverage?

Common reasons for decline or rated offers include: Alzheimer’s disease or other dementias (automatic decline), Parkinson’s disease, multiple sclerosis, stroke with residual deficits, insulin-dependent diabetes with complications, heart failure, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), recent cancer treatment, morbid obesity, and current use of certain medications. Conditions that do not typically disqualify applicants include well-controlled hypertension, controlled Type 2 diabetes without complications, and past cancers considered fully resolved.

Q:  What is the best age to apply?

The ideal window is typically the late fifties to early sixties. At this age, the broadest range of carriers is available, premiums are most favorable, and benefit options are widest. By the mid-seventies, many carriers will not issue new policies, and the overall denial rate after age 70 is approximately 47%. Health conditions that disqualify applicants often develop in the sixties — well before care is needed — which is why early application matters.

Q:  Can I still get coverage if I have some health issues?

Possibly. Many conditions are ratable rather than disqualifying — meaning the policy is issued at a higher premium reflecting the elevated risk. The key is working with an advisor who has access to multiple carriers and knows each carrier’s underwriting guidelines, since standards vary significantly. Mr. Payne has direct experience with the underwriting standards of multiple carriers and can assess eligibility before an application is submitted.

Policy Types & Structures

Q:  What is a hybrid LTC policy and how does it differ from traditional LTC insurance?

A hybrid LTC policy links long-term care coverage to a life insurance or annuity chassis. If care is needed, the policy pays LTC benefits. If care is never needed, a death benefit passes to the policyholder’s beneficiaries. Traditional standalone LTC policies pay benefits only if care is needed; premiums may be lost if care never materializes. Hybrid policies eliminate this “use it or lose it” concern and typically have guaranteed, level premiums rather than premiums subject to carrier-initiated increases.

Q:  What is the California Partnership Program?

California participates in the Long-Term Care Partnership Program, which allows holders of qualifying Partnership-certified LTC policies to protect assets equal to the benefits paid out — dollar for dollar — when applying for Medi-Cal. If your policy pays $250,000 in benefits, you can protect $250,000 in assets and still qualify for Medi-Cal without spending those assets down. Inflation protection is required for applicants under age 76.

Tax Considerations

Q:  Are LTC insurance premiums tax-deductible?

For individuals who itemize deductions, premiums on a federally qualified policy may be deductible as a medical expense subject to age-based annual limits (up to $6,020 for age 71+ in 2026) and the 7.5% AGI floor. Self-employed individuals may deduct eligible premiums as an above-the-line deduction — not subject to the AGI floor — up to the same age-based limits. C-Corporations can deduct 100% of the LTC care portion of a qualifying hybrid premium paid for employee-owners and their spouses, with no age-based limits.

Q:  Are LTC benefits taxable when received?

Generally no. Benefits paid under a federally qualified LTC policy are received income-tax-free, regardless of the actual care costs incurred. This makes LTC insurance one of the most tax-efficient financial products available.

Q:  How does the C-Corp deduction work in practice?

A C-Corporation may deduct 100% of the LTC care portion of a qualifying hybrid policy premium paid on behalf of employee-owners and their spouses — with no age-based limits. In a real example using a joint hybrid policy for an age-50 couple with a $169,917 single premium, the C-Corp tax savings (Federal 21% + CA 8.84% on $101,397 LTC portion) amount to approximately $30,253, reducing the true net cost to approximately $68,036 — while the policy retains a death benefit exceeding $300,000.
