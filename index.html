What does long-term care insurance cover? +

Long-term care insurance pays for care services when you can no longer perform two or more activities of daily living (ADLs) — such as bathing, dressing, eating, toileting, transferring, or continence — or when a licensed healthcare provider certifies a cognitive impairment. Covered care settings typically include your home, adult day care centers, assisted living facilities, memory care units, and skilled nursing facilities (nursing homes).

What is the difference between traditional and hybrid LTC insurance? +

Traditional (standalone) LTC insurance pays a monthly benefit if care is needed. Premiums not used for care are not returned — they are use-it-or-lose-it. Hybrid or asset-based policies combine a life insurance or annuity chassis with an LTC benefit rider. They typically involve a single premium or fixed-payment period and guarantee a death benefit to beneficiaries if LTC benefits are never used. The right structure depends on your age, health, assets, liquidity, and estate planning goals.

Are long-term care insurance premiums tax deductible? +

Yes. Qualified LTC insurance premiums are deductible as a medical expense on IRS Schedule A, subject to annual age-based limits. Business owners who fund premiums through a C-corporation may deduct 100% of premiums as a business expense with no AGI limitation — a significant advantage over personal deductions. Withbert W. Payne can structure your policy to maximize available tax deductions.

How much does long-term care cost? +

The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services estimates that 70% of people turning 65 will need some form of long-term care. National median costs include approximately $9,733 per month for a private room in a nursing home, $5,350 per month for assisted living, and $6,292 per month for home health aide services. California costs typically exceed national medians significantly.
