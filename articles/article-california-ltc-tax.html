California LTC Tax: What’s Actually Happening

California is studying a state-run long-term care program — but no payroll tax, mandate, or opt-out deadline exists today.
Here is what residents and planners need to know.

In the years since Washington State enacted its long-term care payroll tax, other states have been watching closely — and California is no exception. Discussions about a state-run long-term care program have generated attention, concern, and in some cases, premature conclusions about what is in place.

The short answer: as of today, California has not enacted any LTC payroll tax, mandate, or opt-out requirement. What exists is a structured study of the problem — and a policy conversation that is still evolving.

Current Status

Where California Stands Today

California established a Long-Term Care Insurance Task Force to evaluate potential statewide solutions to the growing long-term care financing challenge. The Task Force’s work has focused primarily on two problems: the escalating cost of Medi-Cal long-term care expenditures and the low rate of private LTC planning among California residents.

Among the structures under review:

  • A payroll tax to fund a basic state LTC benefit
  • Mandatory participation for working Californians
  • A potential opt-out pathway for individuals who already own qualifying private LTC insurance

No legislation has been passed. These remain proposals under evaluation, not enacted law.

No opt-out deadline exists. There is no deadline for residents to act, and no window closing.

No requirement to purchase LTC insurance. California residents face no current obligation of any kind.

The Washington Comparison

How This Differs From What Washington Did

Washington State is currently the only state collecting an LTC payroll tax. Workers pay 0.58% of all gross wages, with no income cap, into the WA Cares Fund. The program provides a lifetime benefit, now capped at $36,500 (indexed for inflation) — a figure that covers roughly 3 to 4 months of nursing home care at current rates. Benefit collection began July 1, 2023; benefit payments became available in July 2026.

Washington allowed a one-time opt-out window for residents who owned qualifying private LTC insurance before a specified deadline. That window has since closed permanently. Workers who did not act before the deadline are now enrolled with no exit available, regardless of what private insurance they may acquire later.

“Washington’s experience illustrates the defining feature of any state mandate: the opt-out window, when it closes, closes permanently. Residents who wait to evaluate their options may find they no longer have any.”
The Driving Factors

Why California Is Seriously Considering This

The pressures motivating California’s exploration mirror the conditions that led Washington to act. Several structural factors are making it increasingly difficult for the state to absorb long-term care costs through Medi-Cal alone:

  • Escalating Medi-Cal expenditures. Long-term care is among the largest and fastest-growing categories of Medi-Cal spending. As the population ages, the trajectory is unsustainable without structural change.
  • A rapidly aging population. The share of Californians over 65 is growing sharply, and with it the volume of care events that will need to be financed.
  • Low private LTC ownership rates. Only a small fraction of Californians who would benefit from private LTC coverage actually have it. When those without coverage need care, Medi-Cal typically absorbs the cost after asset spend-down.

These are the same structural pressures Washington faced. They do not resolve on their own, and California’s political leadership is aware that a decision to act is likely a matter of when, not if.

What This Means Now

What California Residents Should Know

At this moment, California residents face no obligation. There is no payroll tax, no opt-out deadline, and no requirement to purchase LTC insurance. For residents who are not currently planning, nothing has changed legally.

But the planning calculus is more nuanced than that — particularly for higher earners and business owners for whom a future opt-out window could carry significant financial consequences. If California eventually follows Washington’s model, the likely structure would include:

  • A mandatory payroll deduction for working Californians
  • A limited state benefit that falls well short of actual care costs for most professionals
  • A narrow, time-limited window to opt out by demonstrating ownership of qualifying private LTC coverage
“The residents most exposed to a Washington-style mandate are high earners who would face the largest payroll deductions while receiving a benefit that covers the smallest fraction of their actual care costs. These are precisely the clients for whom advance private planning makes the most economic sense.”

For California residents who have meaningful income, retirement savings, or assets they wish to protect, the most straightforward observation is this: the planning window is currently open. LTC insurance can be evaluated, underwritten, and placed on its own terms today — without the constraints of a legislative timeline or an opt-out deadline.

When — and if — California enacts a program, that window may narrow significantly. For most professionals, the right time to evaluate long-term care coverage is not the day a California deadline is announced. It is before one exists.

The Bottom Line

No Tax Today — But the Planning Window Is Open

California has not enacted an LTC payroll tax. Residents who have read otherwise have encountered incomplete or premature reporting. There is no deadline, no mandate, and no current obligation.

What does exist is a policy process that is moving in a direction many observers expect will eventually produce legislation. The form, timing, and details of any future California LTC program remain undetermined — which is precisely why the time to evaluate private options is while they are fully within your control.
