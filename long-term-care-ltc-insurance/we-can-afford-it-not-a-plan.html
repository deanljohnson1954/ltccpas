LONG-TERM CARE PLANNING

Why “We Cannot Afford It” Is Not an Adequate Excuse

For families with the means to self-fund care — and every reason to plan for it anyway.

For most of the affluent families I work with, the obstacle to planning for long-term care is not the cost. It is the quiet confidence that the cost is manageable. “We can write that check,” the thinking goes, “so we’re fine.” Often that is true. And it is still not a plan — particularly once you see how little it takes to secure one, and that, structured well, it need not cost you anything at all.

Writing a check is different from planning for the event that triggers it. A care event does not arrive as an invoice. It arrives as a decision — who steps in, where care is delivered, how a household reorganizes itself around it. But before any of that, there is a fact most clients do not believe until they see the figures.

Once the premium is never truly spent, the conversation can turn to what matters.

MOVE THE CONVERSATION FROM RISK TO CONSEQUENCE

Whether you will need care is the wrong question for someone in your position. The better question is what a care event does — not to your balance sheet, which can absorb it, but to the people around you. That is the cost that no level of liquidity solves.

The most meaningful consequences of a care event are rarely financial. They are the ones a checkbook cannot reach.

When a household has no plan, the burden falls where it falls. The most common pattern looks like this:

  • The weight lands on a spouse and adult children — physically and emotionally — long before anyone calls it caregiving.
  • Siblings strain against one another when significant decisions must be made quickly, under stress, with no agreed-upon direction.
  • The family loses control over how and where care happens — the very things that define dignity in later life — because circumstances decide instead.

None of these are problems you opt out of by being able to afford the bill. They are the reasons to plan even when — especially when — the money is not the worry.

DEFINE THE PLAN BEFORE THE PRODUCT

Long-term care planning is often mistaken for nursing-home planning. It is not. It is about preserving comfort, independence, and choice for as long as those are possible. A facility is the last step, not the goal — and there is a great deal of life between remaining at home and that last step.

Before any funding question is worth asking, a plan answers three things, in this order:

  • Who will help provide care? A spouse, adult children, and professional caregivers — each in a different role. Are they able? Are they willing? What will it ask of them?
  • Where care will be provided. Most people want to remain at home as long as they can. Aging in place is the objective; the plan exists to protect it for as long as possible.
  • How care will be funded. Only once the first two are settled does funding become a real decision tied to a real goal.
SEVEN MONTHS OF CARE PAYS FOR A LIFETIME OF IT

Look again at that single premium. For our 50-year-old couple, $203,900 is the cost of 7 months of care at today’s rates — and in exchange, it secures benefits that can last for the rest of two lifetimes. That is the whole idea. You are not spending the money; you are repositioning it. It either returns as care benefits, many times over, or it passes to your heirs intact and then some. The figure rises with age, but the logic holds at every age.

EVERY FAMILY NEEDS A PLAN

Not every family needs insurance to fund care. But every family — particularly those who can comfortably self-fund — owes an honest look at an option that, structured well, returns more than it costs even if it is never used.
