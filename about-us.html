Why Work With a CPA?

Most insurance agents are licensed to sell — but few have the financial training to audit. As a CPA and Chartered Accountant, I evaluate every policy through the lens of tax efficiency, cash flow impact, and long-term financial security. I don't just ask "what does this policy cover?" — I ask, "Is this the most financially efficient way to protect your assets?" That distinction matters enormously for business owners, C-Corp executives, trust beneficiaries, and high-income professionals.

A CPA-trained advisor considers:

  • IRC §7702B qualified LTC premium deductibility for business owners
  • C-Corp 100% deduction strategies for owner-employees
  • Cash flow impact and premium funding options (single premium vs. recurring)
  • Estate planning implications and spousal protection strategies
  • Hybrid life / LTC product analysis vs. traditional standalone policies
  • Tax treatment of benefits received — income-tax-free under current tax law

Long-Term Care Insurance in California

California families face some of the highest long-term care costs in the nation. Bay Area home care can exceed $10,000–$15,000 per month, and skilled nursing can run $15,000 or more. Medicare does not cover extended custodial care, and Medi-Cal covers it only after assets are depleted. Planning early — before health changes make coverage unavailable — is critical. Whether you are searching for long-term care insurance reviews in California, comparing LTC insurance options across top-rated carriers, or looking for an LTC insurance CPA who can evaluate options through a financial lens, Insurance Review Services offers the expertise you need.

Services include long-term care insurance planning, LTC insurance planning for individuals and couples, LTC insurance comparison across top-rated carriers, and analysis of hybrid life/LTC products. Clients across the San Francisco Bay Area, Sacramento, and throughout California are welcome.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Medicare pay for long-term care?
Generally, no. Medicare covers short-term skilled nursing care following a qualifying hospital stay — typically up to 100 days under specific conditions. It does not cover extended custodial care (assistance with bathing, dressing, eating, and mobility), which is what most people mean when they say "nursing home care." Medi-Cal may cover long-term care, but only after assets are substantially depleted. LTC insurance is designed to fill this gap.
What does LTC insurance cover?
LTC insurance pays for care services — in a nursing facility, assisted living community, memory care unit, or your own home — when you can no longer perform two of six Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) or have a cognitive impairment such as Alzheimer's disease. Modern policies are flexible: most benefits today are paid as a monthly maximum you can direct toward any qualifying care setting.
How much LTC coverage do I need?
The right amount depends on your assets, income, health profile, family support, and where you plan to retire. In the San Francisco Bay Area, home care costs often exceed $10,000–$15,000 per month and skilled nursing facilities can run $15,000 or more. Bert will analyze your specific financial picture and help you determine a benefit amount that protects your estate without over-insuring.
When should I buy LTC insurance?
The optimal window is typically ages 50–65. Premiums are lower when you are younger and healthier, and underwriting is more straightforward. Waiting until your 70s — or after a significant health event — can make coverage unavailable or cost-prohibitive. The earlier you plan, the more options you have.
Can couples obtain joint LTC coverage?
Yes. Several carriers offer shared-benefit or joint-life LTC policies that allow spouses or domestic partners to share a pool of benefits. These can be highly cost-effective for couples where one spouse is likely to need care while the other remains healthy. Bert will compare individual and joint options side by side.
What happens if I never use the policy?
With traditional LTC insurance, unused benefits are not returned — similar to auto or homeowners insurance. However, hybrid life/LTC policies (combination products) offer a death benefit to your heirs if you do not use the LTC benefits, and many offer a return-of-premium option. Bert will explain the trade-offs between traditional and hybrid products based on your priorities. See also: Fact vs. Fiction about LTC Insurance.
