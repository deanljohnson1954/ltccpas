When Does Long-Term Care Insurance Begin Paying Benefits?

Understanding the five claim requirements every policyholder and family should know — before a claim occurs.

Most people purchase long-term care insurance hoping they will never need it. But if a claim becomes necessary, understanding exactly what activates your benefits can remove a great deal of uncertainty at an already stressful time.

In most tax-qualified long-term care policies, benefits begin when a licensed health care practitioner certifies that the policy’s claim requirements have been met. The structure of those requirements is broadly consistent across modern policies — but the details vary meaningfully from one contract to the next, and it is the details that determine how quickly a family is reimbursed.

CPA Insight

Many families do not discover how their policy actually works until a claim occurs. Reviewing your coverage before it is needed can prevent confusion, reduce delays, and make certain that expectations match the benefits the contract actually provides.

What Must Be Satisfied Before Benefits Begin

Two Qualifying Triggers. Three Supporting Requirements.

Either the ADL trigger or the cognitive trigger opens the door to a claim — both are not required. Requirements three through five must then be satisfied before benefits are actually paid.

01

Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)

Qualifying Trigger

Benefits generally become available when the insured is unable to perform at least two of the six Activities of Daily Living without substantial assistance:

BathingDressingEating ToiletingTransferringContinence
02

Severe Cognitive Impairment

Qualifying Trigger

Alzheimer’s disease, dementia, or another qualifying cognitive disorder may trigger benefits when substantial supervision is required to protect the insured’s health and safety — even if physical assistance is not yet needed. Cognitive claims are frequently overlooked by families because the insured may still appear physically capable.

03

Certification by a Licensed Health Care Practitioner

A licensed health care practitioner must certify that the insured meets the policy’s benefit trigger. For the ADL trigger, tax-qualified policies require certification that the inability is expected to last at least 90 days. Certification is generally renewed at least once every twelve months for as long as the claim remains open.

04

Elimination (Waiting) Period

Most policies require a waiting period — commonly 30 to 100 days — before benefits begin.

Think of this as a time deductible rather than a dollar deductible.

The mechanics differ by contract. Some policies waive the elimination period for home care entirely. Some count calendar days, while others credit only days on which paid care is actually received. Some require the period to be satisfied once per lifetime; others require it again with each new claim. These provisions rarely appear in marketing material — they live in the contract.

05

Written Plan of Care

A written Plan of Care must be established by a licensed health care professional describing the type, frequency, and level of care required. The care actually delivered — and, in most policies, the providers delivering it — must correspond to that plan in order for benefits to be paid.

What Does Not Trigger a Claim?

Common Misconceptions

Long-term care insurance generally does not begin paying benefits simply because someone:

  • Is hospitalized
  • Has surgery
  • Receives a cancer diagnosis
  • Suffers a heart attack
  • Is receiving Medicare
  • Is simply getting older

Benefits begin only when the policy’s claim requirements are satisfied.

A diagnosis by itself is not a trigger. What the policy measures is function and supervision — what the insured can no longer do safely alone. A serious illness may of course lead to a qualifying condition, but it is the resulting need for hands-on assistance or substantial supervision, not the diagnosis, that opens a claim.

Every Policy Is Different

Benefit triggers, elimination periods, inflation protection, home care provisions, and indemnity versus reimbursement benefits vary by carrier and by policy generation. Two policies issued ten years apart — even by the same carrier — can pay very differently in identical circumstances.

Before a claim occurs, it is wise to understand how your own policy works.

Key Facts

  • Someone turning 65 today has approximately a 70% likelihood of needing some form of long-term care during their remaining years.
  • Most tax-qualified policies pay benefits upon either the inability to perform two of six Activities of Daily Living, or severe cognitive impairment — not both.
  • A licensed health care practitioner — not the insurance company — certifies that the trigger has been met.
  • Understanding your policy early helps families avoid unnecessary delays when a claim finally arises.
