Fact vs. Fiction About Long-Term Care

The most common misconceptions about long-term care insurance — and what the data shows. Each pair presents the myth as it is typically heard, and the evidence-based response.

Myth 1 of 8

Medicare Will Cover My Long-Term Care Costs

✗  Fiction

Medicare covers long-term care, so I don’t need separate insurance.

This is the most widespread misconception in retirement planning.

✓  Fact

Medicare covers acute care only. Custodial care is entirely excluded.

Medicare covers hospitalizations, surgery, physician visits, and short-term skilled nursing after a qualifying hospital stay — up to 100 days, with significant co-pays after day 20. It does not cover ongoing home health aides for custodial care, assisted living, memory care, or adult day programs. These are the services that most long-term care events require.

Myth 2 of 8

My Family Will Take Care of Me

✗  Fiction

My children or spouse will provide care at home. I don’t need a policy.

Most people believe this until the care event begins.

✓  Fact

Family caregiving has financial, physical, and emotional limits.

Professional memory care for advanced dementia commonly runs $12,000–$15,000 per month in the Bay Area. The physical and emotional toll on family caregivers is well-documented, and most cannot maintain full-time employment while providing meaningful care. LTC insurance does not prevent family involvement — it funds professional care, so family members remain family members, not paid caregivers.

Myth 3 of 8

I’ll Self-Insure — I Have Enough Assets

✗  Fiction

I have significant savings. I’ll just pay for care out of pocket if I need it.

Self-insurance is rational for low-probability events with defined costs. LTC is neither.

✓  Fact

Self-insuring a 70% probability, multi-year, six-figure event is a high-risk strategy.

A three-year nursing home stay in California costs over $315,000 at today’s rates. A ten-year dementia event — not unusual for advanced Alzheimer’s — can exceed $1.5 million. For most families, self-insuring this risk means systematically liquidating retirement assets during the years a surviving spouse may need them most. Hybrid LTC policies also allow a full return of premiums if care is never needed — eliminating the “wasted premium” concern.

Myth 4 of 8

I’m Too Young to Think About This

✗  Fiction

Long-term care planning is best addressed closer to retirement.

Many professionals delay because the risk feels distant. The underwriting window does not wait.

✓  Fact

The optimal underwriting window closes well before most people expect.

The ideal window for LTC coverage — broadest carrier selection, lowest premiums, widest benefit options — is typically the late fifties and early sixties. By the mid-seventies, 47% of applicants were declined. Health conditions that disqualify applicants often develop in the sixties, well before care is needed. The underwriting window, once closed, does not reopen.

Myth 5 of 8

LTC Insurance Is Too Expensive

✗  Fiction

LTC premiums are prohibitively expensive and may increase unpredictably.

Traditional standalone LTC policies have experienced premium increases. Hybrid policies are structurally different.

✓  Fact

Modern hybrid policies have fixed, single premiums — and return value even if care is never needed.

Hybrid LTC policies linked to a life insurance chassis have guaranteed, level premiums — often a single lump-sum deposit. If care is never needed, a meaningful death benefit passes to beneficiaries. For a California C-Corp owner, the true net cost of comprehensive lifetime coverage for a couple can be as low as ~$68,000 after tax savings — with a $300,000+ retained death benefit.

Myth 6 of 8

Long-Term Care Mostly Means a Nursing Home

✗  Fiction

If I need long-term care, I’ll end up in a nursing home. I’d rather not plan for that.

The nursing home assumption causes many people to dismiss the planning conversation.

✓  Fact

60% of LTC claims are for home-based care. Most people receive care where they prefer — at home.

Connecticut Partnership data on 6,878 claimants shows 53% of claims are for home health aides and 60% involve home-based or skilled nursing home visits. Assisted living accounts for 29% of claims. Nursing home claims represent 27%. A well-designed LTC policy funds care in whatever setting the individual prefers, including home care with a professional aide.

Myth 7 of 8

The Government Will Cover It Through Medi-Cal

✗  Fiction

If I run out of money, Medi-Cal will pay for my care.

Medi-Cal is a safety net of last resort, not a planning strategy.

✓  Fact

Medi-Cal covers LTC only after all assets have been spent down to eligibility levels.

Medi-Cal does cover long-term care — but only after personal assets have been depleted to program eligibility thresholds. For professionals with retirement savings, a home, or a surviving spouse who depends on those assets, Medi-Cal spend-down represents the destruction of everything retirement planning was designed to protect. California’s Partnership Program allows LTC policyholders to protect assets dollar-for-dollar against Medi-Cal eligibility requirements.

Myth 8 of 8

If I Don’t Use It, I Lose It

✗  Fiction

LTC premiums are wasted if I never make a claim.

The “use it or lose it” concern is legitimate for traditional standalone policies. It does not apply to hybrid structures.

✓  Fact

Hybrid LTC policies are specifically designed so that premiums are never lost.

Modern hybrid LTC policies link long-term care coverage to a life insurance or annuity chassis. If care is never needed, the policyholder’s beneficiaries receive a meaningful death benefit. In some designs, the premium deposit remains accessible during the policyholder’s lifetime. The policy serves both as LTC protection and as a legacy planning tool.
